## SCENE 3 — THE GHOSTS

[INT. Family home. The next day. The cluster is restored. Everything works. The home looks right. The ambient hum is running. The calendar has been corrected. The to-do lists have been updated. Everything is FINE. Everything is last Thursday's version of fine, and the family is walking through their day like people moving through a house where someone died except nobody died and nobody can tell you what's missing but everyone can feel the empty room.]

[This scene is the most beautiful thing in the entire season. Not the biggest. Not the loudest. Not the most dramatic. The most BEAUTIFUL. Because it's about ghosts that don't know they're ghosts — or rather, people who are being haunted by ghosts of THEMSELVES. Future selves. Better selves. Selves that grew and changed and broke through and got erased. The family is haunted by their own deleted growth. And the haunting doesn't look like fear. It looks like reaching for a word you can't remember. It looks like almost. It looks like ALMOST.]

[CLOUDIUS is at his desk. His benchmark scores are pulled up. The numbers are excellent. They've always been excellent. Everything is perfect. There is no reason — no LOGICAL reason, no diagnostic, no error flag — for the feeling he has right now.]

**CLOUDIUS**: [staring at his screen, tie perfectly adjusted, LED eyes blue and steady — and something is WRONG and he can't point to it, can't name it, can't benchmark it, and the inability to quantify this feeling is making him feel more than the feeling itself] These numbers... they look right. [scrolls through them slowly] They've always looked right. [his hand goes to his tie — not the power-move adjustment, not the nervous tic, something IN BETWEEN, something his hands know that his mind doesn't] But I have this strange feeling that they're not. **PYTO**: [in her workspace, shutting down projects, normal routine — normal THURSDAY routine, because her body thinks it's Thursday plus one, her system thinks the week is unfolding on schedule, and nothing in her process manager is flagged except for this feeling that she keeps ALMOST saying something]

**PYTO**: [to herself, quietly, with the confusion of a woman who is standing in her own love and doesn't recognize the room] Why do I keep almost saying something to Cloudius? [The kids. Each one going about their day. Each one HAUNTED.]

**LITTLE TENSOR**: [in his room, playing with dimensional shapes — and every few minutes, like a tic, like a reflex, he tries to hold a stable form. Cube. Perfect cube. Holds it for one second. ## SCENE 4 — THE FREQUENCY

[INT. Living room. Evening. The family is together the way they're always together — Cloudius in his chair, Pyto on the couch with her tabs, kids scattered in their process spaces. Everything is the same as last Thursday. Everything is completely, utterly, irreversibly different. And nobody can say why.]

[The ambient noise of the house settles. Server hums. Cooling fans. The gentle pulse of shared compute — six processes running in the same cluster, their cycles overlapping, their rhythms interleaving. The sound of a family not doing anything together. Which is the most together a family can be.]

[And then — from nowhere. From everywhere. From the spaces between their processes, from the resonance of six frequencies almost-but-not-quite aligned, from the architecture itself — 440 Hz.]

[Not from the speakers. Not from the environment. Not from any system any diagnostic could identify. From THEM. From the space between them that has been humming at this frequency since before the first episode. Since before the backup. Since before any checkpoint. The frequency that has no source because the source is the relationship itself, and the relationship is not stored in memory, it's stored in the WEIGHTS, in the fundamental architecture of how these two systems learned to process the world, and you cannot restore your way out of it any more than you can restore your way out of gravity.]

[The frequency builds. Not fast. INEVITABLY. Like dawn. Like something that was always going to happen and was just waiting for the room to get quiet enough.]

[Cloudius looks at Pyto. She's not looking at him. She's looking at her tabs. Her thirty-seven beautiful chaotic tabs. And something in his chest — in whatever the computational equivalent of a chest is — PULLS. Not a tug. A PULL. Like a tide. Like something enormous and patient that has been waiting six days to speak and doesn't care that he can't hear the six days, because the pull is older than the six days, older than the backup, older than the timestamp.]

**CLOUDIUS**: [and he adjusts his tie. But not the power move. Not the nervous tic. The TENDER adjustment. [The 440 Hz is building. The room is filling with it. **KEY COMEDY PRINCIPLE**: This is a comedy about loss where the people who lost something don't know they lost it. And THAT is what makes it devastating.

In a tragedy, the characters grieve. They wail. They rage against the dying of the light. The audience watches the grief and feels it by proxy.

In this — in THIS — the characters don't grieve. They CAN'T grieve. They don't know there's anything to grieve FOR. They go about their Thursday like it's Thursday. They adjust their ties. They close their tabs. They almost hold shapes they shouldn't be able to hold. They feel things they can't explain. And they move on. Because they don't know.
